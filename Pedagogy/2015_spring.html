INFO 375: TEACHING ASSISTANCE PRACTICUM

Brian W. Carver | Mondays 2-4 | South Hall 205

 

This course is designed to give first-time GSIs some resources aimed to assist them in their teaching, hopefully helping them to break down and better understand the process of learning. They will become familiar with different philosophies, approaches, and techniques of pedagogy, and how these may be applied in the classroom. They will develop skills in the area of curriculum design, lecture preparation and delivery, and designing assignments and evaluations. Overall, GSIs will be better equipped to tackle the challenges they face in the classroom and address the needs of their students.

Week 4: Reading & Writing

The first half of this class will focus on reading. Since reading is often where students get stuck or feel challenged, we will discuss how they might be able to develop better and more efficient ways of reading long articles. The second half of this class will be focused on writing, and offer some resources for students to guide them better in this regard.

 

Readings:

Zamel, V. (1992). Writing one's way into reading. Tesol Quarterly, 26(3), 463-485.

Final Project

 

Options for the final project are offered below. You may choose any one of these.

 

  1. Compose a teaching philosophy statement as you go along, adding to it and editing it every week. This statement should be written such that the reader is clear about what would be most important to you were you to design and teach a course of your own.

  2. Create a teaching journal with your weekly reflections of what worked and what didn’t in a classroom of your choice - what you could have done more of or what you could have done differently.

  3. Compose a memoir that reflects back on your schooling experiences and forward to your professional career as an academic, making references to articles as well as observations from our class.

  4. Take the course that you are currently a GSI for and design a curriculum for it that you think is better, or one that aligns with your teaching philosophy. Create learning objectives for the course overall and for each class session, with aligned participation, in-class exercises, or assignments that you would include, in addition to the syllabus.

  5. If you are not currently teaching (or even if you are), take a course that you are enrolled in and do #4.

  6. Technology in the classroom: Write an essay on how you think technology can and should be integrated into the classroom. What might be the benefits or pitfalls? Cite literature as well as instances from the course you are teaching.

  7. Have a class session of your teaching video recorded and meet with the instructor to discuss. Write a detailed reflection addressing what you learned from watching the video.
